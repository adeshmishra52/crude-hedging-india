# Crude Oil Hedging Policy for Indian Oil Marketing Companies

**A 39-Year Analysis of Brent–INR Exposure and Hedging Strategy for Indian Crude Imports**

📄 **Published on Zenodo:** [doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.20664513](https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.20664513)  
📋 **Status:** Journal manuscript under peer review

---

## Overview

Indian Oil Marketing Companies (IOCs) — IOCL, BPCL, and HPCL — are exposed to two compounding sources of risk on every barrel of crude imported: the international price of crude oil (Brent) and the INR/USD exchange rate. Despite importing over 88% of its crude requirements, India has no formal, systematic hedging framework for this dual exposure.

This paper analyses 39 years of Brent crude price and INR/USD exchange rate data to:
- Quantify the magnitude and volatility of India's aggregate crude import cost exposure
- Evaluate conventional and dynamic hedging strategies across different market regimes
- Develop policy recommendations for Indian OMCs and the Ministry of Petroleum & Natural Gas (MoPNG)

---

## Key Findings

- India's unhedged crude import bill exhibits significantly higher volatility than either Brent or INR/USD in isolation, due to compounding exposure effects
- Static hedge ratios consistently underperform regime-conditioned dynamic strategies across the 39-year sample period
- Geopolitical stress periods (Gulf War, post-Ukraine, COVID supply shocks) represent the highest-cost windows for unhedged Indian OMCs
- A structured hedging framework could materially reduce under-recovery risk for Indian refiners

---

## Methodology

- **Data:** 39 years of monthly Brent crude prices (USD/bbl) and INR/USD exchange rates (1985–2024)
- **Sources:** EIA, World Bank Pink Sheet, RBI DBIE database, PPAC
- **Approach:** Time series analysis, hedge ratio optimisation, regime-based conditional analysis
- **Tools:** Python (pandas, statsmodels, matplotlib)
